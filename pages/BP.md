## Definitions and Fundamentals
- What is a business process? #card
	- A set of logically related tasks performed to achieve a defined business outcome
- What is a process? #card
	- A structured, measured set of activities designed to produce a specified output for a particular customer or market
	- It implies a strong emphasis on how work is done within an organisation
- What two characteristics should processes have? #card
	- Customers
	- Cross organisational boundaries
		- Occur between organisational sub-units
- What is a technique for identifying a business process? #card
	- Value chain method
- How are processes generally defined? #card
	- Beginning and end points
	- Interfaces
	- Organisational units involved
		- Customer unit
- What should High impact processes have? #card
	- Process owners
- What are the three dimensions that may define a process #card
	- Entities
		- What are entities in a process? #card
			- Processes take place between organisational entities.
		- What types of interorganisational processes can exist? #card
			- Interorganizational
			- Interfunctional
			- Interpersonal
	- Objects
		- What role do object play in Processes? #card
			- Processes result in manipulation of objects, physical or informational
	- Activities
		- What are the two types of process activites? #card
			- Managerial
			- Operational
- What are some differences between business processes and organisations? #card
	- Organisations have departments and clear hierarchical structures, processes are more naturally occurring and arbitrary
	- Processes are are invisible
	- Processes are neither named nor described
	- Always cross organizational boundaries
	- Processes occur when people realize that they need to work together to achieve the result promised to the customer
	- Processes cut through traditional organisations
- What are some advantages of business processes over organisations? #card
	- They work over time
	- And produce an output
		- Which means that you can measure the:
			- Costs
			- Output
			- Efficiency
			- Quality
			- Customer Satisfaction
	- Being measurable allows improvement

## Work System Framework
- Why was the work system framework developed? #card
	- Help businesses recognize and understand IT-reliant systems, like business processes in organisations
- What makes the work system framework unique? #card
	- It emphasizes business over IT concerns
	- Provides an outline for describing the business process being studied
	- Identify problems and opportunities
	- Describing possible changes
	- Tracing how changes may affect other parts of the system

## BPM Evolution (Waves)
- How does Dumas (Wave 3) define a business process? #card
	- A collection of inter-related events, activities, and decision points which involve a number of actors and objects and collectively lead to an outcome that is of value to at least one customer
- How does Wave 3 define business process management? #card
	- Body of methods, techniques and tools to discover, analyse, redesign, execute and monitor business processes.
	- BPM inherits: #card
		- Continuous development philosophy
		- Principles and techniques of operations management
		- Lean
		- Six Sigma
	- Combines existing techniques and philosophy with modern Information Technology to optimally align the processes with the objectives of an organisation

	- Cognitive software is re-balancing automation and human interaction
		- How is Cognitive software affecting BPM in wave 4? #card
			- The use of AI allows businesses to move away from duplicated, inconsistent business processes are impede digital innovation

- BP Trends Methodology #card

- What are the assumptions of a process redesign methodology? #card
	- The process redesign project takes place in 5 stages
	- Once completed, the process and associated process management systems will work together to execute processes on a day to day basis.
	- Most implementation phases of most projects would involve other groups like HR and IT
		- To develop components such as training and applications
- When is Process redesign justified? #card
	- It is determined that small changes will not produce the desired result
- When is a Process redesign often triggered? #card
	- Shift in organisational orientation
	- New technology is being implemented and may impact a number of various sub-processes
- IGOE Diagram #card
	- ![What is an IGOE? (Commentary)](https://th.bing.com/th/id/R.e05ce29861f8b1062662dc3aded62c15?rik=ZathngLVvYIKSw&riu=http%3a%2f%2fwww.brcommunity.com%2fimages%2farticles%2fb634-1.png&ehk=45lOWIwZxdUjJiSAQ4EXcxHccJOezGRaqeJM4xZrUkE%3d&risl=&pid=ImgRaw&r=0)
- What is process scoping? #card
	- A method for defining and understanding all information needed to perform a process, including when, why, how, who, and where.
- What does the IGOE diagram represent? #card
	- A complete picture of the components needed to perform a process: Inputs, Guides, Outputs, and Enablers.
- What are Enablers in a process? #card
	- The people, tools, and facilities required to transform inputs into outputs.
- What questions to Enablers answer in a process? #card
	- Who and Where
- Why should Enablers be documented separately from the process flow? #card
	- To avoid confusion between business process flow and system flow.
- What happens when Enablers are included in the transactional flow? #card
	- The model becomes unclear and less useful, confusing business and system flows.
- What are Guides in a Process? #card
	- Policies, procedures, rules, knowledge, experience, measures, and triggering or ending events.
- What role do triggering and ending events play? #card
	- They define beginning and end boundaries of a process
- Why should rules not be treated as process inputs? #card
	- Rules guide decisions, but are not changed by the process itself
- What key decision is needed for effective process analysis? #card
	- Separating state changes from the criteria that causes those changes
- Where do most real process problems originate? #card
	- In the guides/rules. Not inputs and outputs
- How often do process improvement require rule change, instead of flow? #card
	- About 80-85% of the time
- What impact can changing rules have on process cycle times? #card
	- Reduction from days to minutes or seconds
- Why must Guides be separated from the process flow? #card
	- To identify improvement opportunities and maintain a clear, maintainable model.
- What is the first step in defining process boundaries and project scope? #card
	- Identifying and agreeing on the beginning and ending events of the process.
- Who are stakeholders in a process? #card
	- Sources or destinations of information or items used or produced by the process.
- How are stakeholders identified? #card
	- By asking process participants where information comes from and where it goes.
- What is a process context diagram? #card
	- A diagram showing the process’s relationship with internal and external stakeholders.
- What is the purpose of identifying major problem areas early? #card
	- To accurately define the focus and scope of an improvement project.
- What is a “health check” technique? #card
	- A high-level root cause analysis using red, yellow, and green indicators.
- What do red, yellow, and green indicators mean in a health check? #card
	- Red: serious problem, Yellow: minor problem/opportunity, Green: no change needed.
- What is the outcome of reaching consensus using a health check? #card
	- A clearly defined project scope and commitment of resources.
- In *process scoping*, what is the **first activity** used to define the boundaries of a process? #card
	- Identify and agree on the **triggering (beginning) and ending events** of the process.
- After identifying the beginning and ending events during *process scoping*, what is the **next step**? #card
	- Identify the **stakeholders** that interact with the process.
- In process scoping, how are stakeholders identified once process boundaries are agreed upon? #card
	- By asking where information/things come from and where they go to after the process is complete.
- What diagram is created after stakeholders are identified during process scoping? #card
	- A process context diagram.
- In process scoping, why is the process context diagram created before detailed process analysis? #card
	- To clearly show process boundaries and external relationships and prevent scope creep.
- Once the process context is established in process scoping, what is the next major activity? #card
	- Identify the major problem areas in the process.
- What technique is used during process scoping to identify major problem areas at a high level? #card
	- A health check.
- In the process scoping health check, what is the sequence of evaluation? #card
	- Apply red, yellow, and green indicators to process areas.
- After completing the health check in process scoping, what decision is made? #card
	- Agreement on the focus and scope of the improvement project.
- What must happen before resources are committed in process scoping? #card
	- Stakeholders reach consensus on the project scope.
- In process scoping, at what point does detailed process modeling (e.g., IGOE) become appropriate? #card
	- After boundaries, stakeholders, and scope have been agreed upon.
- Why is defining events and context done before analyzing guides and enablers in process scoping? #card
	- Because clear boundaries and scope are required before meaningful analysis can occur.

## Process Redesign
- Despite its acceptance, how is Business Process Redesign (BPR) typically characterized in practice? #card
	- More art than science.
- Who primarily develops BPR design methodologies? #card
	- Consulting firms.
- What is a common characteristic of consulting-developed BPR methodologies? #card
	- They are proprietary.
- What do most existing BPR methodologies emphasize? #card
	- Project management and organizational issues.
- What key challenge do many BPR methodologies fail to address? #card
	- The technical challenge of designing a radically improved process.
- According to Valiris and Glykas, what is a major limitation of existing BPR approaches? #card
	- The lack of a systematic, step-by-step approach for process redesign.
- What criticism do Sharp and McDermott make about BPR methodologies? #card
	- They do not explain how to move from the “as-is” process to the “to-be” process.
- What is the “ATAMO procedure” in the context of BPR criticism? #card
	- “And Then, A Miracle Occurs” — a sarcastic reference to missing redesign guidance.
- Who identified four main dimensions affected by redesign measures? #card
	- Brand and Van der Kolk.
- What are the four dimensions of process redesign effects? #card
	- Time, cost, quality, and flexibility.
- What are the ideal outcomes of a successful business process redesign? #card
	- Reduced time, reduced cost, improved quality, and increased flexibility.
- What is a key insight of Brand and Van der Kolk’s redesign model? #card
	- Improving one dimension can negatively affect another.
- Give an example of a trade-off in process redesign. #card
	- Adding reconciliation tasks may improve quality but reduce timeliness.
- What is the “devil’s quadrangle”? #card
	- A model illustrating trade-offs among time, cost, quality, and flexibility.
- Why is the model called the “devil’s quadrangle”? #card
	- Because difficult trade-offs often must be made between competing dimensions.
- What does the gray diamond in the devil’s quadrangle represent? #card
	- A neutral area where impacts are negative.
- What does an impact outside the gray diamond signify? #card
	- A positive impact on the redesign dimension.
- What best practice is illustrated in the devil’s quadrangle figure? #card
	- Task elimination.
- Why is awareness of trade-offs important in process redesign? #card
	- Because improving performance in one area often weakens another.
- What is Control Relocation? #card
	- Moving control and checking activities closer to the customer to reduce errors and improve satisfaction, but with higher fraud risk
- Main benefit and drawback of control relocation? #card
	- Benefit: fewer errors and higher customer satisfaction. Drawback: increased fraud probability and lower yield
- What is Contact Reduction? #card
	- Reducing or combining interactions with customers or third parties to cut waiting time and errors
- Risks of reducing customer contacts? #card
	- Loss of essential information or excessive data exchange, harming quality or increasing cost
- What does Integration in process design mean? #card
	- Aligning processes with customers or suppliers for more efficient joint execution
- Main drawback of integrated processes? #card
	- Increased mutual dependence and reduced flexibility
- What is the Order Types best practice? #card
	- Separating different kinds of work into distinct processes or subflows to improve efficiency
- Risk of distinguishing many order types? #card
	- Increased coordination problems and reduced flexibility
- What is Task Elimination? #card
	- Removing tasks that add no customer value, often control or redundant tasks
- Key risk of task elimination? #card
	- Reduced service quality due to missing checks
- What is Order-Based Work? #card
	- Removing batch processing and periodic activities to speed up individual orders
- Trade-off of eliminating batch processing? #card
	- Faster processing vs. loss of economies of scale and higher system availability costs
- What is Triage in business processes? #card
	- Splitting tasks into alternatives or merging alternatives into one task to better match order and resource characteristics
- Risk of excessive specialization via triage? #card
	- Reduced flexibility, inefficiency, and monotonous work
- What is Task Composition? #card
	- Combining small tasks or splitting large tasks to balance setup time, flexibility, and quality
- Main trade-off in task composition? #card
	- Large tasks reduce setup time but limit flexibility; small tasks increase flexibility but raise setup time
- What is Resequencing? #card
	- Reordering tasks to reduce cost, setup time, or unnecessary work
- What is the Knock-Out principle? #card
	- Ordering checks by highest termination probability and lowest effort first
- Benefit of knock-out ordering? #card
	- Minimizes average execution cost
- What is Parallelism? #card
	- Executing tasks concurrently to reduce throughput time
- Risks of increased parallelism? #card
	- Higher costs, complexity, errors, and reduced flexibility
- What is the Exception best practice? #card
	- Designing processes for normal cases and isolating exceptions
- Trade-off of isolating exceptions? #card
	- Better efficiency for normal flow vs. increased process complexity
- What is Order Assignment? #card
	- Assigning as many steps as possible of an order to the same worker
- Risk of order assignment? #card
	- Reduced flexibility and longer waiting times if the worker is unavailable
- What is Flexible Assignment? #card
	- Assigning tasks to preserve future flexibility, often favoring specialists
- Downsides of flexible assignment? #card
	- Uneven workload and limited skill development
- What is Centralization? #card
	- Treating distributed resources as centralized using workflow systems
- What does Split Responsibilities aim to avoid? #card
	- Tasks shared across departments that cause conflict and neglect
- What are Customer Teams? #card
	- Cross-functional teams handling complete order types
- What is Numerical Involvement? #card
	- Minimizing the number of people and departments involved in a process
- Risk of reducing numerical involvement? #card
	- Loss of expertise and routine
- What is a Case Manager? #card
	- A single accountable person for managing an order or customer
- What is the Extra Resources best practice? #card
	- Increasing capacity to reduce queue time, at higher cost
- What does Specialist–Generalist balance address? #card
	- Trade-off between efficiency and flexibility
- What is Empowerment? #card
	- Giving workers decision-making authority and reducing middle management
- Risk of empowerment? #card
	- Poor decisions and costly rework
- What is Control Addition? #card
	- Adding checks to improve quality and reduce rework
- How does control addition contrast with task elimination? #card
	- Control addition adds checks; task elimination removes them
- What is Buffering? #card
	- Subscribing to information updates instead of requesting them on demand
- Main drawback of buffering? #card
	- High subscription and storage costs
- What is Task Automation? #card
	- Using technology to perform tasks faster, cheaper, and more consistently
- Main downside of task automation? #card
	- High development cost and reduced flexibility
- What is Integral Technology? #card
	- Using new technology to remove physical or logistical constraints
- What is the Trusted Party best practice? #card
	- Using verified results from another organization instead of redoing checks
- Risk of relying on trusted parties? #card
	- Dependence on external quality and reduced flexibility
- What is Outsourcing? #card
	- Having another party perform part or all of a process
- Difference between outsourcing and trusted party? #card
	- Outsourcing executes tasks at runtime; trusted parties reuse past results
- What is Interfacing? #card
	- Using standardized interfaces with customers and partners to reduce errors.

## Sociotechnical Perspective
- What is the sociotechnical perspective in IS? #card
	- A view that considers both technical artifacts and social actors, emphasizing outcomes emerging from their interaction.
- What does the sociotechnical perspective not privilege? #card
	- Neither the technical nor the social system alone.
- What outcomes does the sociotechnical perspective value? #card
	- Instrumental outcomes (efficiency, productivity) and humanistic outcomes (well-being, equality, freedom).
- Where did sociotechnical thinking originate? #card
	- Post-WWII field studies in the British coal-mining industry by the Tavistock Institute.
- What dominant view did sociotechnical thinking challenge? #card
	- The idea that technology is an external force determining organizational structure and behavior.
- How did sociotechnical thinking influence IS as a discipline? #card
	- It helped bridge socially oriented and technically oriented approaches to organizational problems.
- What workplace vision did early IS sociotechnical thinking promote? #card
	- A humanized and efficient workplace.
- What risk did sociotechnical IS thinkers warn against? #card
	- Subjugating humans to machines.
- What is IS change? #card
	- A deliberate change to technical and organizational subsystems handling information.
- How does IS change affect work systems? #card
	- By embedding new IT components that reconfigure how work is executed and coordinated.
- Why is IS change difficult? #card
	- Due to path dependence, habitualization, cognitive inertia, and high complexity.
- Why must IS change be planned and deliberate? #card
	- Because work systems have low malleability and strong interdependencies.
- What is a gap in a sociotechnical system? #card
	- A structural misalignment that threatens system performance and viability.
- What causes gaps in IS work systems? #card
	- Component variation, system failures, learning, replacements, or gradual drift.
- What is a critical incident? #card
	- Any event that generates a gap in the system.
- What are interventions? #card
	- Deliberate actions aimed at removing or mitigating gaps in sociotechnical systems.
- Why do interventions sometimes fail? #card
	- Due to cognitive failures, system complexity, poor execution, or randomness.
- What are unintended effects of interventions? #card
	- Second- and third-order effects leading to path dependence or system paralysis.
- Why are emotions important in ERP implementations? #card
	- They strongly influence engagement, acceptance, and job performance.
- What gap exists in traditional IS change research? #card
	- Limited attention to users’ affective (emotional) responses.
- What was the PERP system? #card
	- An ERP system implemented for student administration at a South African HEI.
- What affective pattern dominated users’ ERP experience? #card
	- High engagement but low satisfaction.
- Which negative emotions were common in ERP adoption? #card
	- Frustration, resistance, anxiety, resignation, and low confidence.
- How are emotions classified in IS studies? #card
	- By activation (high/low) and pleasantness (positive/negative).
- What triggers negative affective responses during ERP adoption? #card
	- High workload, unclear roles, insufficient training, and poor planning.
- How do negative emotions affect IS outcomes? #card
	- They reduce group performance and affective acceptance.
- What factors influence users’ affective responses in ERP projects? #card
	- Implementation choices, facilitating conditions, system perceptions, and job impact.
- How did parallel system use affect perceptions? #card
	- It increased perceptions of inefficiency.
- What reduced anxiety during ERP adoption? #card
	- Peer support.
- What increased frustration in ERP implementation? #card
	- Poor planning, lack of training, and weak support.
- How does job impact influence system perceptions? #card
	- Increased learning improves perceived ease of use; restricted access increases perceived inefficiency.
- What should organizations do with emotional responses? #card
	- Address them openly by creating trustful spaces over time.

## Job Design
- What does traditional job design focus on? #card
	- The relationship between the job and the individual performer.
- Why is job design considered nested? #card
	- It exists within organizational, national, and global contexts.
- What was the goal of major job design models across cultures? #card
	- Enhancing employee self-worth and well-being.
- What is the core idea of Hackman and Oldham’s job enrichment model? #card
	- Designing jobs to increase intrinsic motivation and satisfaction.
- Which job characteristics increase meaningfulness? #card
	- Skill variety, task identity, and task significance.
- What role does autonomy play in job design? #card
	- It increases responsibility for outcomes.
- How does feedback affect job performance? #card
	- It supports self-regulation and motivation.
- How does sociotechnical job design differ from Taylorism? #card
	- It rejects excessive standardization and routine as the sole source of efficiency.
- What is optimized in sociotechnical job design? #card
	- The joint integration of social and technical systems.
- At what level does sociotechnical job design primarily operate? #card
	- The group level.
- What group-level mechanisms support sociotechnical job design? #card
	- Group autonomy, shared responsibility, group feedback, and task meaningfulness.
