- What is an enterprise system? #card
  - Software used for business management, often adopted as part of digital transformation initiatives.
- Why are enterprise systems important in digital transformation? #card
  - They integrate and standardize business processes across an organization.
- What is Materials Requirements Planning (MRP)? #card
  - A system used to plan production and raw material purchases for future periods.
- What information does an MRP system use? #card
  - Supplier lead times and bills of material (BOMs) or product recipes.
- How did MRP systems evolve into MRP II systems? #card
  - By adding extra modules to support broader manufacturing resource planning.
- What is MRP II? #card
  - Manufacturing Resource Planning, an extension of MRP with additional manufacturing-related modules.
- What characterizes the first ERP era? #card
  - Separate best-of-breed systems with added modules built on MRP/MRP II foundations.
- Why is the first ERP era called “best-of-breed”? #card
  - Organizations used specialized systems for different functions rather than a single integrated system.
- What defines the second ERP era? #card
  - Tightly integrated modules across multiple business functions.
- How are ERP systems formally defined? #card
  - Standard software packages that integrate processes from multiple business functions.
- Which core business functions are typically covered by ERP systems? #card
  - Finance, accounting, sales, operations management, purchasing, and human resource management.
- Give an example of an ERP system. #card
  - SAP ERP.
- How are traditional ERP systems often described? #card
  - As monolithic “back-office” software.
- What triggered the evolution toward postmodern ERP? #card
  - Integration of ERP with other enterprise systems.
- Which systems were integrated with SAP ERP in the SAP Business Suite? #card
  - PLM, CRM, SCM, and SRM.
- What additional capabilities were introduced in postmodern ERP systems? #card
  - User-friendly portals, business intelligence (BI), and analytics.
- How does Gartner define postmodern ERP? #card
  - Loosely coupled, easier to use, cloud-based, highly integrated systems with high process agility.
- How do leading companies use ERP in the postmodern era? #card
  - For non-differentiating processes, augmented by agile cloud-based applications.
- Why are companies reducing ERP customization? #card
  - To free resources for innovation and new technologies.
- Which emerging technologies are associated with postmodern ERP? #card
  - AI, conversational UIs, and robotic process automation (RPA).
- Why is SAP S/4HANA considered a postmodern ERP? #card
  - It supports cloud, on-premise, and hybrid deployment and uses SAP Fiori UI.
- What is the focus of the fourth ERP era? #card
  - A transformative mindset extending beyond the enterprise.
- Why does Gartner propose replacing the term ERP? #card
  - ERP no longer reflects the broader, capability-driven nature of modern systems.
- What is the new term proposed by Gartner? #card
  - Enterprise Business Capabilities (EBC).
- What are the six hallmarks of Enterprise Business Capabilities? #card
  - AI-driven, customer-facing, data-centric, enabling, consumable, and people-augmented.
- What does “consumable” software mean in EBC? #card
  - Software that is reliable and requires minimal customization.
- How does the focus of ERP change in the EBC era? #card
  - From optimizing processes to delivering outcomes and business value.
- Why is benefits realization important in ERP IV? #card
  - Because success is measured by value and outcomes, not just process execution.
- Which technologies support the shift to Enterprise Business Capabilities? #card
  - Process automation, robotics, IoT, conversational interaction, data processing, and decision support.
- Why is data critical in ERP IV? #card
  - Data is essential for AI and must be carefully managed as “AI’s food.”
- What trend has occurred among ERP suppliers over the last decade? #card
  - Substantial market consolidation.
- Which companies are among the top ERP suppliers by license revenue? #card
  - SAP, Oracle, Sage, Infor, and Microsoft.
- Give examples of open-source ERP systems. #card
  - Odoo and ERP5.
- How has the ERP market changed in terms of organization size? #card
  - It has expanded from large enterprises to include small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs).
- How are ERP suppliers responding to diverse market needs? #card
  - By targeting niche vertical segments with specialized ERP products.
- What delivery model is common for modern ERP solutions? #card
  - Software as a Service (SaaS) cloud-based ERP solutions.
- Which ERP system is used in the ESEFA curriculum? #card
  - SAP S/4HANA.
- How are core modules identified in SAP S/4HANA? #card
  - Using two-letter module codes.
- What does the SAP module FI stand for? #card
  - Financial Accounting.
- What does the SAP module CO stand for? #card
  - Controlling.
- Which SAP modules fall under Logistics? #card
  - SD (Sales & Distribution) and MM (Materials Management).
- Why are SaaS ERP solutions attractive to SMBs? #card
  - They reduce upfront costs, simplify deployment, and scale more easily.
- Which framework commonly describes the phases of ERP implementation? #card
  - The ITIL (Information Technology Infrastructure Library) application management process.
- How many phases are involved in the ITIL-based ERP implementation process? #card
  - Six phases.
- What is the purpose of the requirements determination phase? #card
  - To identify and document business and system requirements.
- What happens during the design phase of ERP implementation? #card
  - The system and business processes are designed to fit organizational needs.
- What is the goal of the build and test phase? #card
  - To configure, develop, and test the ERP system.
- What is included in the deploy phase? #card
  - All planning required before the organization goes live with the ERP system.
- What marks the start of the operate phase? #card
  - The “go live” event.
- What is the focus of the operate phase? #card
  - Ensuring the ERP system is available and usable for end users.
- What activities are included in the optimize phase? #card
  - Ongoing evaluation, benefits realization, and managing upgrades and new releases.
- What is a business blueprint in ERP implementation? #card
  - A detailed description of how the organization intends to run its business processes using the ERP system.
- During which ERP implementation phase is the business blueprint created? #card
  - The design phase.
- How many ERP data types make up a business blueprint? #card
  - Four.
- What is the first ERP data type in the hierarchy? #card
  - Organizational units/data.
- What does organizational unit data represent? #card
  - The structure of the organization, such as company codes, plants, and departments.
- What is master data in ERP systems? #card
  - Relatively stable data used across multiple business processes (e.g., customers, vendors, materials).
- What does the business process data type include? #card
  - Business processes and their associated tasks or transactions.
- What are ERP transactions? #card
  - System activities that execute specific tasks within a business process.
- What is the fourth ERP data type in the business blueprint hierarchy? #card
  - Reports.
- What is the role of reports in ERP systems? #card
  - To provide information for monitoring, analysis, and decision-making.
- Why are ERP data types organized hierarchically? #card
  - Because higher-level data (organizational units and master data) underpin business processes and reporting.
- What does organizational data represent in an ERP system? #card
  - The structural representation of the business.
- How is a business modeled in SAP during implementation? #card
  - By creating organizational units and assigning them to an organizational structure through customizing.
- What is customizing in SAP? #card
  - Configuration of the system to reflect the organization’s structure and business processes.
- What is a client (or tenant) in SAP? #card
  - A self-contained unit with its own master data and database tables.
- What is the highest organizational element in SAP? #card
  - The client (tenant).
- How is a SAP client identified? #card
  - By a 3-digit key.
- What is a company code? #card
  - An independent accounting unit for which financial statements are produced.
- Why are company codes required? #card
  - Because financial reporting is legally required at the company level.
- How is a company code represented in SAP? #card
  - By a four-character code (e.g., ZM00).
- Can a company code have more than one chart of accounts? #card
  - Yes, for financial reporting purposes.
- What is a plant in SAP? #card
  - An organizational unit within a company where goods are produced, stored, or services are delivered.
- Give examples of what a plant can represent. #card
  - Manufacturing facility, warehouse, distribution center, or service office.
- What is a storage location? #card
  - A subdivision within a plant used to differentiate material stocks.
- Give examples of storage locations. #card
  - Raw materials, finished goods, semi-finished goods, trading goods.
- What is a shipping point? #card
  - The organizational unit responsible for shipping goods.
- What is the relationship between shipping points and plants? #card
  - A shipping point can serve one or more plants; a plant can have zero, one, or many shipping points.
- What is a purchasing organization? #card
  - The unit that negotiates purchasing conditions with suppliers.
- At which level are pricing conditions set? #card
  - Purchasing organization level.
- What is a purchasing group? #card
  - A buyer or group of buyers responsible for purchasing activities.
- Why is the purchasing group important if it cannot be assigned to a purchasing organization? #card
  - It is mainly used for reporting purposes.
- What is a sales area? #card
  - A combination of sales organization, distribution channel, and division.
- Can a sales area belong to more than one company code? #card
  - No, it can belong to only one company code.
- What is a sales organization? #card
  - The unit responsible for selling and distributing goods and services.
- How many company codes can a sales organization belong to? #card
  - Only one.
- Can a company code have multiple sales organizations? #card
  - Yes.
- What is a distribution channel? #card
  - The means by which products or services reach customers (e.g., retail, wholesale, internet).
- What is a division in SAP? #card
  - An organizational unit used to group materials or services into product groups.
- What is master data in an ERP system? #card
  - Data shared across systems and business processes and used repeatedly.
- How is master data handled during ERP implementation? #card
  - Uploaded from legacy systems and maintained centrally after go-live.
- Give examples of master data types. #card
  - Customers, suppliers, materials (inventory items), employees.
- Why can master data records be large? #card
  - They contain many attributes used across multiple processes.
- When are business processes defined in an ERP implementation? #card
  - During the design phase.
- How are business process tasks executed in an ERP system? #card
  - Through transactions.
- Give examples of business process tasks. #card
  - Creating a purchase order, receiving goods, creating a sales order.
- Why must master data exist before transactions can be executed? #card
  - Transactions reference master and organizational data.
- What is transactional data? #card
  - Data created as a record of business events during process execution.
- At what level is transactional data stored? #card
  - Document level.
- What does document-level transaction data include? #card
  - Headers, footers, and line items.
- What is reporting data mainly derived from? #card
  - Summarized transactional data.
- What types of data are included in reporting data? #card
  - Transactional, master, and organizational data.
- Give an example of reporting data. #card
  - Monthly number of purchase orders per supplier or per company code.
- Why is organizational data foundational in ERP systems? #card
  - Because master data, transactions, and reporting all depend on the organizational structure.
- How have software applications traditionally been classified? #card
  - As either custom-built or off-the-shelf software.
- Why do ERP systems not fit neatly into the custom-built vs off-the-shelf classification? #card
  - They are commercial packages but require significant configuration and long-term supplier relationships.
- What type of relationship do ERP adopters typically have with ERP suppliers? #card
  - Long-term partnerships.
- How do ERP systems evolve over time? #card
  - Through new releases that fix bugs and add new functionality.
- How can ERP adopters influence future ERP releases? #card
  - By logging development requests and participating in user groups.
- Why is ERP implementation and use fundamentally different from custom-built systems? #card
  - Because adopters depend on supplier-driven development and ongoing maintenance.
- What must be done before an ERP package can be used by an organization? #card
  - It must be configured to fit organizational structures and business processes.
- During which ERP implementation phase does configuration mainly occur? #card
  - The build and test phase.
- What is table configuration in ERP systems? #card
  - Setting table parameters to alter system functionality without coding.
- What is table configuration called in SAP? #card
  - Customizing.
- Does ERP configuration require programming? #card
  - No, it does not require coding.
- Why do organizations spend significant effort on ERP configuration? #card
  - Because ERP packages support many organizational possibilities through configuration.
- What trade-off does ERP configuration usually involve? #card
  - Balancing how the organization wants to work with how the ERP system allows it to work.
- How does ERP programming differ from configuration? #card
  - Programming involves writing or modifying code, while configuration adjusts parameters.
- What is ERP code modification? #card
  - Changing the ERP package’s source code to support unique business processes.
- Is ERP code modification recommended? #card
  - No, it is strongly discouraged.
- Why is ERP code modification risky? #card
  - Supplier updates can overwrite changes, requiring costly and risky manual integration.
- What programming options are preferred over modifying ERP core code? #card
  - Adding custom objects in designated areas or developing interfaces.
- Are programming options supported by ERP suppliers? #card
  - Generally no, they are not fully supported.
- What are the disadvantages of ERP programming options? #card
  - Reduced upgradeability, dependency on developers, and limited access to supplier innovations.
- When should ERP programming be considered? #card
  - Only when business process redesign is unacceptable and sufficient budget exists.
- How can organizations reduce the need for ERP programming? #card
  - By carefully selecting an ERP system that fits organizational requirements.
- What is meant by the divided software life cycle of ERP systems? #card
  - The shared responsibility between adopters (implementation and operation) and suppliers (development and maintenance).
- Which activities are primarily handled by ERP suppliers? #card
  - Software development, maintenance, enhancement, and support.
- Which activities are primarily handled by ERP adopters? #card
  - Implementation, configuration, operation, and usage.
- Why is ERP configuration preferred over programming? #card
  - It preserves upgrade paths, reduces risk, and allows adopters to benefit from supplier updates.
